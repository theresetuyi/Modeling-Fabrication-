# 1. Foundations of Modeling & Fabrication
# Introduction
On Day 1, we explored the foundations of modeling and fabrication and how digital design connects to physical production. I learned that modern design is computational and material-aware, meaning fabrication is not a final step but a factor that shapes decisions from the beginning. Modeling was described as more than creating form  it represents logic, behavior, and design intelligence.

I studied the design-to-fabrication continuum, which includes designing, modeling, prototyping, fabricating, and evaluating. This process is iterative, not linear, and failure is considered part of learning. I also learned about different fabrication paradigms such as subtractive, additive, formative, and hybrid methods. Each method affects how a design is produced and must be considered early in the workflow.
Materials and tolerance were another key topic. Real-world fabrication introduces imperfections, deformation, and limits. Designers must anticipate these constraints and design for them rather than assume perfect conditions. Parametric thinking was introduced to control variation without redesigning everything, and prototypes were discussed as research tools that test assumptions and support decision-making.
A practical component of the day involved building a documentation website using MkDocs with the Material theme. I structured pages to record modeling workflows, fabrication logic, and reflections. This taught me that documentation is part of the design process and allows others to understand and reproduce the work. Publishing through GitHub Pages also introduced version control and public knowledge sharing.
The session ended with a discussion on ethics and sustainability. I reflected on material waste, energy use, and the responsibility designers have when creating physical systems. This reinforced that not everything that can be fabricated should automatically be produced.
# References & Inspiration
The lecture materials emphasized the relationship between design intent and fabrication reality. The framework of the design-to-fabrication continuum inspired me to view modeling as a research tool rather than only a visualization step. The idea that failure is a learning mechanism also influenced how I approach prototyping.
The MkDocs documentation workflow served as inspiration for professional technical communication. It demonstrated how structured documentation can preserve knowledge and support collaboration. Ethical considerations around sustainability encouraged critical thinking about responsible design and production.
