# 4. Materials & Fabrication Methods
# Overview: Understanding Materials Through Making
Day 4 focused on understanding how material selection and fabrication methods directly influence design quality, functionality, durability, and sustainability. Rather than treating materials as passive elements, this session emphasized that designers actively design material processes. The interaction between material properties and fabrication techniques determines what is feasible, efficient, and reliable in manufacturing. 
# Importance of Materials and Fabrication Methods
Materials define the structural integrity, environmental impact, and lifecycle of a product, while fabrication methods influence precision, form, cost, and scalability. Effective design requires aligning material choice with the intended fabrication process to achieve optimal performance and minimize waste. This alignment ensures that design decisions are both practical and sustainable. 
# Material Properties and Selection Strategy
Material selection is driven by performance requirements rather than appearance alone. Key properties considered include strength, flexibility, density, and thermal or electrical behavior. Designers must also consider the intended function, environment of use, and the fabrication method to be applied. Strategic material selection improves durability and reduces production errors.
# Wood as a Fabrication Material
Wood is a natural, renewable, and versatile material widely used in digital fabrication. Its grain direction significantly affects strength and aesthetics, making orientation a critical design consideration. Wood is sensitive to moisture and requires proper finishing to maintain durability.
# Common fabrication processes include:
•	CNC routing for complex shapes
•	Laser cutting for detailed and precise components
Typical applications: furniture design, architectural models, and rapid prototyping. 
# Plastics: Lightweight and Adaptable Materials
Plastics offer a wide range of properties depending on their type. Thermoplastics such as PLA, ABS, and acrylic are commonly used due to their adaptability and ease of fabrication. They are well suited for 3D printing, laser cutting, and CNC milling. However, plastics are highly sensitive to heat, which can cause deformation if not properly controlled during fabrication. 
# Metals: Strength and Precision
Metals are essential for applications requiring high strength, durability, and precision. Aluminum, steel, and brass are commonly used in fabrication, each offering different advantages in weight, corrosion resistance, and conductivity. Metal fabrication typically requires robust tools and precise control, increasing cost and energy consumption compared to other materials. 
# Composites and Engineered Materials
Composites combine multiple materials to achieve enhanced performance characteristics such as high strength-to-weight ratios. Examples include fiberglass, carbon fiber, and laminated wood. While composites offer superior performance, they present challenges in fabrication complexity and recyclability, requiring deeper material understanding. 
# Fabrication Approaches: Subtractive vs Additive Manufacturing
Two primary fabrication approaches were explored:
•	Subtractive manufacturing: Material is removed from a solid block (e.g., CNC milling, laser cutting). This method offers high precision and surface quality.
•	Additive manufacturing: Objects are built layer by layer (e.g., 3D printing), allowing complex geometries and customization.
Choosing the appropriate approach depends on material type, design complexity, and production goals. 
# Material Behavior During Fabrication
Materials respond differently to heat, force, and speed during fabrication. These factors influence deformation, melting, stress, and tool wear. Understanding material behavior helps prevent defects such as warping and surface damage, leading to higher quality outputs. Designing with material behavior in mind is a key advanced fabrication skill. 
# Sustainability and Material Efficiency
Sustainable design was a core theme of Day 4. Designers must minimize waste, reduce energy use, and prioritize recyclable materials. Digital fabrication supports sustainability through optimized material usage and localized production. Effective design balances cost, durability, performance, and environmental responsibility across the product lifecycle.
